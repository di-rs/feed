Using the U.S. Interstate Highway System's 90/10 federal/state funding ratio as a case study, this article explores how interfaces — funding structures, software defaults, policy design — subtly make choices for users by reshaping the terrain around decisions. Refusal becomes costly and acceptance becomes inertia, creating cascade effects and irreversible path dependencies written into the physical or digital world. Understanding these default-path mechanics is essential for designers, policymakers, and anyone building systems that influence human behavior at scale.
