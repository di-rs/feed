A reformed command-and-control engineering leader argues that centralized decision-making is the least efficient way to build software, framing trust as a "low-latency network" that eliminates bottlenecks and speeds up the entire team. By becoming the required approval point for every decision, the author inadvertently capped the team's velocity to one person's bandwidth while stealing engineers' growth and ownership. Shifting to distributed leadership — where engineers own decisions closest to the work — produces teams that move faster and care more deeply about outcomes than any manager can enforce.
